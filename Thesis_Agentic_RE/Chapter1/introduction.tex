\chapter{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Software reverse engineering often require analysts to combine decompilation, string recovery, capability triage, configuration extraction, and other artifact-driven inspection steps. That workflow is tool-rich and evidence-driven; however, it is also time-consuming and difficult to automate well. This thesis studies whether an agentic system that explicitly orchestrates multiple tools can produce better binary understanding than weaker baselines that rely on simpler prompting or less structured analysis flows. The question is increasingly relevant because public reporting and federal complaint data suggest that AI-enabled cyber operations and AI-assisted cybercrime are already appearing outside laboratory demonstrations \cite{anthropic2025espionage,fbi2025ic3}.

The system presented in this thesis is centered on Model Context Protocol (MCP)-style tool orchestration rather than a single monolithic agent. In its maintained form, the workflow combines a light preparatory stage, deterministic presweeps, planning, specialist workers, optional validation, and final reporting, with structured access to Ghidra, supporting static-analysis utilities, and artifact-recovery services. The thesis therefore addresses both systems questions and empirical questions: how such a workflow should be designed for reverse engineering, why those design choices matter, and whether they measurably improve task performance.

\section{Research Questions}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Does a tool-orchestrated agentic workflow improve binary-analysis task performance relative to weaker baselines?
\item Which workflow surfaces appear to matter most in the evaluated experiments: worker specialization, worker prompt shape, response scope, or architecture emphasis?
\item How consistent are those effects across executables and task types, and when do overall averages hide heterogeneous behavior?
\item What robustness and orchestration failure modes remain even when the workflow is tool-augmented?
\end{enumerate}

\section{Thesis Scope and Expected Contributions}

This thesis makes four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A modular agentic reverse-engineering system that separates preparation, planning, specialist evidence collection, optional review, and reporting so those roles can be varied and measured explicitly.
\item MCP-mediated integration of Ghidra, supporting static-analysis tools, and artifact-recovery services, providing structured tool access rather than ad hoc manual interaction.
\item A binary-analysis evaluation harness built around sample-task pairs, baseline-first sweeps, repeated runs, and judge-driven scoring, designed to make workflow comparisons reproducible.
\item A focused empirical investigation of whether tool-augmented agents outperform weaker baselines on reverse-engineering and malware-understanding tasks, with attention to both aggregate performance and executable-level heterogeneity.
\end{enumerate}
