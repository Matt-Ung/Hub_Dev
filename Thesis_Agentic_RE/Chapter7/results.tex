\chapter{Results and Analysis}
\label{sec:results}

This chapter separates three kinds of evidence by evidentiary weight. First, the archived broad and decoder-depth studies are used as development-stage evidence: they helped narrow the final comparison set and exposed workflow design problems, but they are not the main basis for final claims. Second, the repaired final held-out experiment is used for the main performance comparisons because it restores complete coverage across all seven configurations. Third, the unrecovered final sweep is retained only as a robustness caveat, because it shows an infrastructure failure mode that the repaired aggregate would otherwise hide.

\section{Development-Stage Archive Evidence}

The archived broad and decoder-depth figures are still useful, but they should be read as methodology-shaping artifacts rather than as final effect estimates. Their main value is that they show which workflow changes were worth carrying into the held-out evaluation and which ones exposed design problems before the blind final comparison was launched. The broad pilot sample set is summarized in Table~\ref{tab:broad-sweep-samples}, and the variant labels used across the archive and final studies are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:evaluation-variants}.

\subsection{Broad Coverage Pilot}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering

\caption{Archived broad-pilot ranking. This figure is used here as a descriptive development artifact: it shows which intervention families separated from the baseline strongly enough to justify follow-up study, but it is not treated as a thesis-grade estimate of general effectiveness.}
\label{fig:broad-repaired-ranking}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:broad-repaired-ranking} supports two narrow conclusions. First, the pilot was sufficient to rule out some directions as uninteresting for the final comparison set. In particular, the final thesis sweep did not continue treating reduced tool access as a central variable, because that direction behaved more like an artificial handicap than an informative workflow comparison. Second, prompt-surface and worker-topology changes were large enough in the pilot to justify carrying them forward into deeper testing.

The broad pilot does \emph{not} support a stronger claim than that. It is best read as a screening step over the authored benchmark. It helps explain why the final sweep focused on worker subagents, worker prompt shape, response scope, and architectural emphasis, but it does not by itself establish that any of those variables generalize to harder held-out malware families.

The archived decoder-depth study supports a stronger but still limited interpretation. Compared with the broad pilot, it provides clearer evidence that workflow changes can matter differently on a deeper reverse-engineering task family than on easier overview-oriented tasks. Figure~\ref{fig:decoder-depth-ranking} shows that worker prompt shape and worker topology can change performance on packed and stripped decoder-oriented cases rather than merely changing report style. Figure~\ref{fig:decoder-depth-reliability} shows that not every additional layer helps. The validator-heavy pipeline behaves as a concrete negative case because its collapse is visible as a reliability and coverage failure rather than only a low score. The exact stripped and UPX-packed executables used in that case study are listed in Table~\ref{tab:depth-sweep-samples}, and the decoder-specific variant labels are defined in Table~\ref{tab:evaluation-variants}.

This case study therefore motivated two design choices for the held-out evaluation. The final sweep kept worker-topology and prompt-shape comparisons, because the decoder-depth figures suggested they could matter on richer tasks. It did not elevate the validator-heavy pipeline into the final comparison set, because the development evidence suggested that its failure mode was structural rather than merely unlucky on one sample.

\section{Final Held-Out Evaluation}

Unless otherwise noted, performance comparisons in this section refer to the rebuilt repaired experiment, which preserves the benchmark scope and configuration set while folding in successful recovery results for tasks that initially failed for infrastructure reasons. (The exact experiment identifier and artifact provenance are documented in Appendix~\ref{app:reproduction-commands}.) The unrecovered sweep remains important and is discussed separately in Section~\ref{sec:final-failure-analysis}, because it reveals a real robustness limitation of the workflow. The held-out sample aliases and evaluator-side technique summaries are listed in Table~\ref{tab:final-heldout-samples}, and the configuration labels that appear in the figures are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:evaluation-variants}.

\subsection{Overall Performance After Recovery}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{experiment_results/final_eval_overall_ranking.png}
\caption{Repaired final held-out ranking across all seven configurations. This figure uses the rebuilt repaired experiment so that all configurations have full coverage. The highest descriptive mean score belongs to \texttt{worker\_prompt\_shape:artifact\_focused}, but none of the overall shifts reached the thesis confidence target of $p \le 0.05$.}
\label{fig:final-overall-ranking}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{experiment_results/final_eval_effect_size_summary.png}
\caption{Effect-size summary for the repaired final held-out comparison relative to baseline. This figure matters because the repaired experiment supports descriptive ranking, but the inferential question is whether any overall shift is large and stable enough to treat as more than a sample-limited difference.}
\label{fig:final-effect-size-summary}
\end{figure}

Figures~\ref{fig:final-overall-ranking} and~\ref{fig:final-effect-size-summary} support several direct findings. First, the final repaired comparison does \emph{not} show a statistically significant overall winner at the thesis confidence target. The best descriptive overall mean score is produced by \texttt{worker\_prompt\_shape:artifact\_focused} at \texttt{82.382}, compared with the baseline at \texttt{78.096}, but the replicate-level permutation test yields \texttt{p = 0.5}. Second, four of the six non-baseline variants underperform the baseline descriptively on overall mean score, and none of those degradations are statistically significant either. Third, the effect-size view shows why the chapter should stop short of a stronger claim: the descriptive winner moves in a positive direction, but the repaired final run still provides only three repetitions per configuration and none of the comparisons meets the thesis significance target.

These findings answer the first research question in a constrained way. The final repaired run does \emph{not} justify claiming that any alternative workflow is superior overall on the held-out benchmark. It does, however, establish that the evidence-first worker-prompt overlay (\texttt{artifact\_focused}) is the strongest descriptive candidate in the current design space, while the remaining alternatives either appear neutral-to-negative overall or depend heavily on which executable is being analyzed. That executable dependence motivates the finer-grained analysis in the next subsection.

\subsection{Executable-Level Heterogeneity}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{experiment_results/final_eval_cross_executable_heatmap.png}
\caption{Cross-executable score-delta heatmap for the repaired final held-out experiment. Positive cells indicate improvement over the baseline on that executable and negative cells indicate degradation. This view matters because every non-baseline configuration in the repaired final run is marked as mixed and masked by average in the executable-consistency summary.}
\label{fig:final-cross-executable-heatmap}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:final-cross-executable-heatmap} shows why the overall mean is not enough. All six non-baseline configurations are heterogeneous across the five executables. In the executable-consistency summary, every non-baseline row is labeled \texttt{mixed}, and every one is flagged as \texttt{masked\_by\_average=true}. That is not a rhetorical caveat; it is a direct property of the repaired final dataset. The paged per-executable score panels generated from the same repaired experiment retain the repetition-level variance detail and are included in Appendix~\ref{app:final-per-executable-panels}, but the heatmap is the more legible thesis-scale summary and is therefore the figure kept in the main text.

The concrete pattern is easy to summarize. Baseline remains strongest on \texttt{sample2.exe} and \texttt{sample5.exe}. \texttt{worker\_prompt\_shape:empty} is the top descriptive performer only on \texttt{sample1.exe}, and even there the gain over baseline is modest at \texttt{+1.19}. \texttt{worker\_prompt\_shape:artifact\_focused} is strongest on \texttt{sample3.exe}, where it improves mean score by \texttt{+21.427} relative to baseline. \texttt{architecture\_preset:model\_augmented} ties for the best score on \texttt{sample4.exe} and improves by \texttt{+11.903} there, but it is also the worst-performing configuration on \texttt{sample1.exe} with a delta of \texttt{-17.856}. These are sample-dependent reversals, not minor fluctuations around the same answer. Table~\ref{tab:final-heldout-samples} is useful alongside this subsection because the alias names alone do not tell the reader which kinds of behaviors or analysis challenges each sample was meant to stress.

Two conclusions follow directly from the data. First, the final benchmark is heterogeneous enough that the configuration with the best global mean can still be a poor choice for specific executables, a finding that would be invisible in aggregate-only reporting. Second, the final repaired dataset supports \emph{conditional} findings more confidently than \emph{global} ones: some interventions help on certain executable profiles while hurting others. This answers the third research question more clearly than the first or second one. The held-out results show that executable-level heterogeneity is not a side note; it is central to how these workflows should be interpreted.

The next interpretive step is plausible but not fully proven: the executables that benefit from \texttt{artifact\_focused} or \texttt{model\_augmented} may be the ones where deeper control-flow reconstruction or evidence organization matters more than broad default coverage. The current experiment is suggestive on that point, but with only five executables it is better to treat that as a hypothesis for future stratified evaluation than as a settled causal explanation.

\subsection{Efficiency and Repeated Tool Use}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{experiment_results/final_eval_redundant_tool_usage_by_executable.png}
\caption{Per-executable semantic duplicate tool-call volume in the repaired final held-out experiment. The figure summarizes repeated tool activity within tasks, not proven waste. It is useful because it separates overall duplicate volume from the narrower same-source loops discussed in the text.}
\label{fig:final-redundant-tool-usage}
\end{figure}

The repaired final run also supports a more operational analysis of efficiency. Figure~\ref{fig:final-redundant-tool-usage}, together with the tool-redundancy summaries generated from the same repaired experiment, shows that repeated tool activity is not evenly distributed across executables or configurations. In total semantic duplicate volume, \texttt{sample5.exe} is the largest hotspot with \texttt{501} duplicate calls across the seven configurations, followed by \texttt{sample2.exe} with \texttt{364}. However, that broad volume should not be interpreted as pure waste. Much of the \texttt{sample5.exe} activity comes from cross-stage or cross-source overlap around \texttt{ghidra\_program\_info}, especially across preflight, deterministic presweeps, and worker stages.

The narrower same-source or likely-wasteful view tells a different story. When the analysis is restricted to repeats currently flagged as likely wasteful, \texttt{sample2.exe} becomes the largest hotspot with \texttt{26} likely-wasteful duplicate calls, followed by \texttt{sample3.exe} with \texttt{20}. Tool-family summaries show that this behavior is dominated by two patterns. The first is repeated \texttt{todo\_read} state lookups, which account for \texttt{41} likely-wasteful duplicate calls overall. The second is a smaller but still visible set of Ghidra rescans, especially \texttt{ghidra\_program\_info} (\texttt{12}) and \texttt{ghidra\_imports} (\texttt{11}). Concrete hotspots include the model-augmented architecture on \texttt{sample3.exe}, where \texttt{todo\_read} was called \texttt{22} times with \texttt{19} duplicates and \texttt{7} same-source repeats from \texttt{preflight.manager} and \texttt{reporter.manager}; and the single-generalist variant on \texttt{sample2.exe}, where \texttt{ghidra\_program\_info} was called \texttt{33} times with \texttt{30} duplicates across preflight, presweeps, and worker stages.

The complexity interpretation should remain cautious. Using baseline mean task wall-clock duration as a rough proxy for per-sample analysis difficulty, the repaired final data show only a moderate descriptive association with total semantic duplicate volume and little evidence of a clean relationship with likely-wasteful same-source loops. The longest baseline case, \texttt{sample5.exe}, does have the highest total duplicate volume, but \texttt{sample2.exe} has the highest likely-wasteful count despite a lower baseline wall-clock duration than \texttt{sample1.exe}, \texttt{sample3.exe}, or \texttt{sample5.exe}. The evidence therefore supports a limited claim: executables that are more ambiguous or artifact-heavy may induce more repeated tool activity overall, but the current data do not support a simple causal claim that executable complexity alone explains avoidable duplicate work.

Variant-level efficiency patterns are likewise mixed. The brief response-scope variant produces the highest total likely-wasteful duplicate count (\texttt{19}), while the baseline is lowest at \texttt{4}. Yet repeated calls are not a complete explanation of runtime or score behavior. The evidence-focused worker-prompt variant is the only final variant whose mean wall-clock duration exceeds the baseline, but it is not the most duplication-heavy configuration. Conversely, the single-generalist and Ghidra-focused variants run faster than baseline while still producing more likely-wasteful duplicates. The actionable conclusion is therefore narrow: the data point to concrete opportunities for stage-level safeguards around repeated \texttt{todo\_read} loops and for better reuse of Ghidra program or import context across preflight, presweeps, and worker stages, but they do not justify treating duplicate counts as a standalone explanation for either score differences or runtime.

\subsection{Robustness Caveat from the Original Held-Out Sweep}
\label{sec:final-failure-analysis}

The repaired aggregate is the right artifact for comparing final scores, but the original unrecovered held-out sweep still matters as a methodological caveat. It exposed a real first-pass robustness problem concentrated on a subset of the harder samples, especially when Ghidra-heavy worker assignments accumulated too much shared context before completion. That matters because some one-shot failures were infrastructural rather than purely analytical, so the unrecovered sweep alone would understate the workflow's usable performance on the held-out benchmark.

That caveat should not dominate the results narrative, however. The main thesis question in this chapter is what can be learned from the completed final comparisons once recovery restores coverage. The unrecovered sweep is therefore best treated as context for interpreting those repaired comparisons, not as the central empirical result. The detailed outcome counts, per-executable failure figures, and assignment-failure breakdown are moved to Appendix~\ref{app:heldout-failure-accounting}, and Chapter~\ref{sec:limitations} discusses what they do and do not imply about robustness.

\section{Discussion and Claim Boundaries}

\subsection{Findings Directly Supported by the Data}

The repaired final experiment supports five direct findings. First, no non-baseline configuration achieved a statistically significant overall score improvement at the thesis confidence target. Second, \texttt{worker\_prompt\_shape:artifact\_focused} is the strongest descriptive overall candidate in the repaired final comparison, but that claim is descriptive rather than confirmatory. Third, executable-level behavior is strongly heterogeneous: every non-baseline configuration is mixed across the five executables, and every one is marked as masked by average in the executable-consistency summary. Fourth, repeated tool activity is concentrated on a subset of executables and tools rather than being evenly distributed across the benchmark; the largest likely-wasteful hotspots come from \texttt{todo\_read} loops and a smaller set of Ghidra rescans. Fifth, the original unrecovered held-out sweep exposed a real workflow robustness problem dominated by host-worker context overflow on Ghidra-heavy assignments.

\subsection{Plausible Interpretations}

The results also suggest, but do not prove, several interpretations. The first is that evidence-first prompt shaping may help most on executables where the bottleneck is organizing and grounding many competing artifacts rather than simply producing a concise report. The second is that architecture-level changes such as \texttt{model\_augmented} may be acting as conditional strategies rather than universal improvements: they help on some executables and clearly hurt on others. The third is that the held-out benchmark is stressing two capabilities at once, namely reverse-engineering quality and orchestration robustness. The failure analysis makes it plausible that some of the apparent performance difference between configurations is mediated by how well they keep worker contexts tractable, not only by how well they reason after the context is assembled. The efficiency summaries suggest a related operational hypothesis: some repeated work is likely caused by missing stage-to-stage reuse or state-loop controls rather than by analytical necessity alone.

\subsection{Open Uncertainties and Measurement Gaps}

Several uncertainties remain open. The final comparison still covers only five held-out executables and three repaired repetitions per configuration, so the executable-level patterns should be treated as strong case evidence rather than as a stable estimate of population-level behavior. The significance tables reinforce that caution: none of the overall score shifts reached the thesis significance threshold, and no multiple-comparison correction is applied in the exploratory task-level tables. In addition, the repaired experiment necessarily combines original runs with targeted recovery runs. That is appropriate for obtaining full benchmark coverage, but it means the final aggregate should be read as the best-available completed comparison under the evaluated workflow rather than as a pristine one-shot sweep.

The tool-usage summaries are also useful but limited. They identify repeated-call hotspots and they now distinguish same-source repeats from cross-source overlap within a task. However, they still do not attach every tool call to a specific work item inside a reused worker slot. That means the current instrumentation can support statements such as ``this task and source produced many repeated calls'' or ``same-source repetition exists here,'' but it cannot yet prove that each repeated call was truly unnecessary. The same repaired final study also uses a single \emph{default analysis} task per held-out executable and keeps the \texttt{repo\_default} model profile fixed across all seven configurations. As a result, the current efficiency analysis cannot cleanly separate task-type effects from executable effects or attribute redundancy differences to model-family changes. Those measurement limits are discussed more fully in Chapter~\ref{sec:limitations}.

Taken together, the repaired final results support three evidence-backed conclusions. First, no single configuration can be claimed as globally best with statistical confidence at the thesis significance target. Second, workflow choices matter materially at the executable level: \texttt{worker\_prompt\_shape:artifact\_focused} is the strongest descriptive overall candidate and produces the largest positive delta on \texttt{sample3.exe} ($+21.4$ over baseline), but the same configuration does not dominate uniformly. Third, executable-level heterogeneity is large enough that average-only reporting would be misleading: every non-baseline configuration is flagged as \texttt{masked\_by\_average} in the consistency summary. These findings frame agentic reverse engineering as a workflow-selection and robustness challenge rather than a prompt-tuning problem with a single optimal answer.
