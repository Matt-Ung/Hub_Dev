\abstract

Malware analysis through reverse engineering remains a labor-intensive process that requires analysts to combine evidence from disassembly, decompilation, string and configuration recovery, capability triage, and related artifact inspection. In practice, analysts move among several tools and intermediate representations rather than relying on a single unified view of the binary, which makes the problem difficult to automate cleanly \cite{kunwar2024sok,mattei2022reusability,yakdan2016johnny}. Although large language models have improved substantially in code understanding and tool use, it remains unclear how well they can support binary analysis when the relevant evidence is fragmented across specialized tools rather than contained in source code. Public reporting and federal complaint data further suggest that AI-enabled cyber operations are already moving beyond laboratory demonstrations \cite{anthropic2025espionage,fbi2025ic3}.

This challenge is especially apparent in malware analysis, where samples routinely employ evasion and obfuscation techniques designed to hinder analysis, including anti-analysis checks, packed or encrypted artifacts, staged behavior, dynamically generated code, and environment-sensitive execution \cite{kunwar2024sok,raitsis2025obfuscation}. Thus, malware reverse engineering serves as a demanding stress test for tool-using LLM systems, because RE success routinely depends on structured evidence gathering, cross-tool synthesis, and flexible reasoning capabilities for handling uncertainty.

This thesis is organized around three questions: what capability gaps remain in an agent's ability to analyze obfuscated binaries, which workflow variables materially affect analysis performance, and whether the resulting analyses are properly backed by artifact evidence. The implemented system uses a staged pipeline with a light preparatory stage, deterministic presweeps, planning, specialized workers, optional validator review, and final reporting rather than a single monolithic agent. These stages have structured access to Ghidra, static-analysis utilities, unpacking support, and artifact-recovery services, following an analysis-first design closer to program-analysis-assisted approaches such as ReSym than to text-only prompting \cite{ghidramcp2025,ghidraplugin2025,xie2024resym}. The goal is to study how staged reasoning, explicit tool access, and evidence review affect tasks such as behavior reconstruction, configuration decoding, packing analysis, and malware-oriented triage.

To make the workflow both usable and measurable, the thesis studies the same pipeline in two modes: an analyst-facing interface for interactive inspection of pipeline progress and tool activity, and an evaluation harness that runs controlled sample-task experiments with rubric-guided LLM-as-a-judge scoring and graph generation. These judge outputs are treated as proxy evaluation signals rather than human annotations or objective ground truth. In the final held-out comparison against five blind malware-simulation executables, the repaired experiment suggests that the system can often produce usable static analyses on stripped, packed, and otherwise obfuscation-heavy binaries, but not with universal reliability or depth. One-shot held-out runs exposed real capability gaps, including context-bloat failures, request-budget failures, repeated tool-use hotspots, and incomplete later-stage recovery on harder cases. Across the tested workflow variables, no alternative achieved a statistically significant overall improvement over the maintained baseline, although worker prompt shape and architecture produced meaningful executable-specific shifts. The LLM-judge evidence-grounding scores and technical summaries further suggest that some completed runs support artifact-backed analysis under the repository rubric, but that grounding quality remains configuration-sensitive. The final evidence therefore supports a narrower conclusion: in this setting, agentic reverse engineering behaves more like a capability-gap, workflow-selection, and evidence-grounding problem than a search for one universally best configuration.
